Case Study

Fijord: From Meeting Chaos to Shipped Tickets

Designing an AI-powered product management tool that transforms discovery calls into evidence-backed work items.

Role Senior Product Designer & Design Engineer
Timeline February 2026
Team Solo

The Problem

Product managers, designers, and researchers spend hours after every user interview doing the same tedious work: re-watching recordings, copying quotes into documents, synthesizing findings, and writing tickets manually.

The pain is real but fragmented. No single step is unbearable, but the cumulative tax is significant. And critically, the evidence trail disappears. By the time a ticket reaches engineering, the original user quote is long gone.

"When I wasn't in that meeting... I didn't have full context on why we're even doing this."

Product Manager, Amazon

The Opportunity

I challenged myself early: "This is free with AI. Anyone can paste a transcript into ChatGPT and get tickets."

That pushed me to identify what ChatGPT can't do:

  • Track patterns across 10+ meetings over months
  • Auto-detect "this theme keeps coming up"
  • Maintain persistent links between tickets and original evidence
  • Generate living briefs from accumulated context

Fijord's opportunity: Be the connective tissue between meetings and tickets, with evidence that compounds over time.

Design Process

Information Architecture

I mapped the full user journey from raw input to shipped outcome:

Evidence → Signals → Epics → Tickets → Shipped Work
↑ |
└──────── Backlinks ────────────────────┘

Key architectural decisions:

  • Meetings live globally but feed into multiple Epics. One interview might surface onboarding issues AND pricing confusion.
  • Signals are pre-Epics. When the AI detects a recurring theme, it surfaces as a Signal. Once strong enough, the user promotes it to an Epic.
  • Epics contain the workflow: Discovery → Scope → Roadmap → Brief.
  • Backlinks close the loop. Every exported ticket links back to Fijord showing the full evidence trail.

Protecting the Quick Win

A key tension emerged: building power-user features while preserving the "30-second magic" that hooks new users.

Two entry points on the dashboard

"Process a new meeting" for immediate value. Paste transcript, get tickets.
"Upload to library" for power users. Feed the Signals engine.

New users experience magic immediately. Over time, they discover the deeper value as patterns emerge.

Key Design Decisions

1. Terminology Matters

User testing revealed confusing language:

  • "Pattern" felt abstract, so I changed it to "Signal"
  • "Call" felt narrow, so I changed it to "Meeting"
  • "Patch" confused PMs, so I changed it to "Solution"

2. Evidence-First Problem Cards

Rather than showing tickets first, I designed problem cards that lead with evidence. The green left border on quotes became a consistent pattern, and users quickly learned to scan for evidence.

3. Drawer Pattern for Transcripts

Initially I designed a two-column layout: transcript on left, extracted content on right.

Problem: The transcript dominated attention, but it's not the value. The extraction is.

Transcript lives in a slide-out drawer

Main view focuses entirely on what Fijord extracted. Users can reference the transcript when needed, but it doesn't compete for attention.

4. Status Without Color Overload

Initial designs used colored left borders to differentiate Epic statuses. I pushed back:

  • Multiple colors fragment visual hierarchy
  • Status badges already communicate state
  • Green progress bars should be the only color accent

Final design: Clean cards with status communicated through badges only.

Interactive Prototypes

Epics Page Interactive: try hovering on cards
Epic cards show progress, status, metrics, and owner at a glance. Status badges use semantic colors while the rest of the UI stays minimal.
Signal Detail Page Interactive: explore the evidence groupings
Signals show strength, evidence quotes grouped by meeting, and a timeline of how the signal emerged. Users can promote strong signals to Epics.
Meeting Detail Page Interactive: click "View transcript" to open the drawer
Focus on extracted value: problems and connections. Full transcript lives in a drawer, accessible but not dominant.

Outcomes

Designed & In Development

Full transcript → tickets flow, Linear/Jira integrations, backlinks, dashboard, signals, and epic views

User Validation

"That full lineage from messy call data to the ticket, that's what speaks to me." (Amazon PM)

What I Learned

  • Protect speed-to-value. Power features are the moat, but the quick win is the hook.
  • Evidence > AI magic. Users don't trust AI summaries alone. Showing source quotes builds confidence.
  • Terminology is UX. Renaming "Patterns" to "Signals" wasn't cosmetic. It changed how users understood the feature.
  • Design engineer velocity. Being able to implement my own designs cut iteration cycles from days to hours.
